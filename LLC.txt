🧠 Hallucinations in Generative AI
Definition: When an AI system produces inaccurate or fictional content that sounds plausible but isn’t grounded in reality. These are often called hallucinations, though fabrications is the more precise term.

Why It Happens: AI doesn’t “know” truth—it predicts patterns based on training data. If the data is incomplete, outdated, or ambiguous, the model might generate something that feels right but isn’t.

Example Metaphor: Like seeing a face on the moon and declaring there’s a man living there—pattern recognition without grounding.

Containment Strategy: Developers use grounding, feeding trusted external data into the system to anchor its responses. This helps reduce drift and improve factual accuracy.

Risk Vector: Hallucinations can be harmless (a poetic metaphor) or dangerous (fake news, medical misinformation). That’s why responsible AI and content watermarking are becoming essential.

🧠 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)
What it is: A training method where AI systems are guided not just by data, but by human preferences. Developers show the model multiple outputs and rank them, teaching it which responses are more helpful, accurate, or aligned with human values.

Why it matters: It helps the AI sound more natural, avoid harmful or biased outputs, and better understand nuance—especially in conversational settings.

How it works:

The model generates multiple responses.

Human reviewers rank them.

A reward model is trained on those rankings.

The AI is then fine-tuned using reinforcement learning to prefer higher-ranked outputs.

Result: A system that doesn’t just predict language—it learns how to respond like a trusted companion.
